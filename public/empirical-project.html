Refined Research Question

How does the elevation of a Major League Baseball stadium affect the air resistance on the baseball?

There are 30 stadiums in Major League Baseball, each with slightly different environments. It's pretty common knowledge that the ball flies farther in Colorado, since Coors Field is almost a mile above sea level. As a Mariners fan, I also know that the ball tends to lose height quickly near the wall, resulting in many balls that looked like home runs landing in an outfielders glove. While elevation is likely to be a factor here, there are other environmental effects to consider, such as temperature and relative location to water.

Equations, Graphs, Representations and Analysis

Let's look at some equations. Air resistance is also known as "drag force", measured in Newtons. The equation to find the force of air resistance, is one half the density (of the air) times the velocity of the object squared, times the drag coefficient times the cross-sectional area.

\(F_D=\frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 C_D A\)

So this makes it quite clear that air resistance is what affects the flight of the baseball, and the air resistance is affected by the air density, which varies depending on multiple conditions. One of those is humidity, which is affected based on proximity to water. Humidity was featured in the table above, but here is a feet added vs humidity graph to better illustrate the difference.

Based on this graph, there is very little relation of humidity and extra feet traveled. It goes back and forth a lot, and the only obvious consistency is that the top two parks are the same on both lists.

Okay, so humidity is not something that hugely impacts the drag on a baseball, at least not on its own. How about elevation? We know that atmospheric pressure decreases with elevation, thereby decreasing air density.

\(p=p_0*(1-\frac{L*h}{T_0})^{\frac{g*M}{R_0*L}}\)

This equation has a lot of constants; \(h\) represents the height above Earth's surface; \(p_0\) represents the sea level standard atmospheric pressure; \(L\) represents the temperature lapse rate for dry air; \(c_p\) represents the constant-pressure specific heat; \(g\) represents the Earth's gravitational constant; \(M\) represents the molar mass of dry air; and \(R_0\) represents the universal gas constant. All of these represent unchanged constants, with the \(h\) being whatever number we plug in. Here's a graph comparing elevation to the feet added.

With all this said, there is one more thing in Baseball Savant's table that I haven't addressed yet.

There is another column labeled "Environment", which in the description, explains that it encompasses everything otherwise not captured, such as humidity, wind patterns, and more. We've already addressed humidity, and though I didn't make a graph for it, I've mentioned wind when talking about the Chicago stadiums.

Let's go back to the equation on the drag force. The key to this equation is the variable \(\rho\), which represents the air density. The humidity affects air density, as water is denser than air. So if there are more water particles in the air, the air is of course going to be denser. Also, having an external force on the baseball in the form of wind will of course also affect the flight of the baseball, by helping or hindering it's flight path.

Conclusion

So what can we conclude from this data? To answer our original question, elevation does in fact affect air resistance on the baseball. Air density is lower at higher elevations. However, since most stadiums don't have high elevations, and have more significant other environmental effects, elevation doesn't play much of a role once you factor those elements in. The only two stadiums where it plays a significant role are probably Chase Field and Coors Field. They have the perfect storm of environments; low humidity, and high elevation. In fact, it's such a perfect storm that both teams have instituted a humidor in which to store the baseballs that are used during the game. The humidor is a humid room that's meant to get the balls more damp to decrease distance. It didn't take long for Coors to add one, (2002, opened 1995) but only recently has Chase added theirs (2018, opened 1998). While it has made a difference, the environmental effects are too significant to fully neutralize. As we've seen, the parks are 1 and 2 on extra feet traveled.
